Unlike many ancient religions that relied primarily on oral traditions, rituals, and priestly roles, Christianity centered on:

## Printing instead of writing

Before printing books were extremely expensive. It took years to copy a book. A monk could only produce a couple of pages a day. With printing the printing laymen where capable of printing up to 2500 pages a day. Almost a hundred times increase in speed. Until printing became available books where simply too rare and expensive to have around all the time whihc meant that you had to remember the contents of the book by head. Only the teacher had the book available during lecturing the students and the student had to learn the contents by listening to the teacher and remembering the contents by head. In the time of Erasmus when printing became more available the way of knowledge transfer for students changed from listening to reading. Widespread monoteims came with books. Protestantism came with printing. Priniting also changed the formal written language from Latin to the general local native language. Both the books and printing as "language enhancement tools" make scare expertise more widely available and easy accessible and democratized humanity. Below you see handwritten book pages

Printing as solution supercharged the renaissance

**Amplified existing trends** - Ideas that were already circulating among educated elites could now spread faster and wider. A humanist text no longer required months of hand-copying; hundreds of identical copies could be produced quickly.

**Standardization** - Printed books meant everyone read the same version of Plato or Aristotle, enabling more productive scholarly debates and building cumulative knowledge.

**Broader literacy** - As books became cheaper and more available, literacy rates increased beyond clergy and aristocracy, expanding the audience for Renaissance ideas.

**Preservation** - Classical texts were less likely to be lost. Multiple copies in multiple locations meant knowledge was more secure.

**The Reformation connection** - Printing had its most dramatic impact with the Protestant Reformation (1517 onwards). Luther's 95 Theses and vernacular Bible translations spread like wildfire thanks to printing, fracturing Christian unity in ways that would have been impossible with hand-copied manuscripts.
